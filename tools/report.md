# Dossier Readability Report — `cornell-university.pdf`

**Reviewer perspective:** the student (Toru), reading the dossier to decide whether to apply.
**Verdict:** the *content* is thorough and well-researched, but the *format* fights the reader.
The three questions a student actually opens this to answer — **Can I get in? Can I afford it?
What do I actually have to do?** — all require reading dense paragraphs two or three times over,
and the single make-or-break warning is the easiest thing to miss.

---

## 1. Overwhelm & density

- **Every section is one dense block of prose.** 16 sections, almost all a single fat paragraph.
  There is no white space and no way to skim — to find one fact you must read the whole wall.
- **Everything is bolded, so nothing stands out.** Sections 4, 11 and 12 bold nearly every clause.
  When half the text is bold, "bold" stops meaning "important" and the eye has nowhere to land.
- **Overwritten phrasing adds length, not information** — "gorges but grind", "you set your own
  temperature", "not a lonely pressure-cooker", "not a brochure line", "maxed". It reads like it's
  trying to impress the student rather than inform them.

## 2. It talks *about* the student, not *to* them

- **Third-person "Toru / his" is jarring to read about yourself** — "his #1 priority", "for Toru",
  "a Malaysian student is far from alone". It feels like an internal report *about* the student, not
  a guide *for* them.
- **It cites "#1 priority" / "#2 priority" but never lists them.** The priorities (funding, hands-on)
  are referenced repeatedly but never stated up front, so the ranking is confusing without outside
  context.

## 3. Heavy repetition

- **The funding weakness appears ~4 times** — Snapshot (§1), §11, and twice in §15 — often the *same
  phrase* ("weakest funding structure of his six US finalists").
- **"SAT required and not yet sat" appears in §1, §4, §12 and §15.** Same for the halal info, the
  "need-aware for internationals" point, and "apply directly to the College of Engineering".
- **§15 (Why here / why hesitate) re-summarizes the whole document** — an entire second read of
  content already covered.
- **Slogans repeat as if new** — "any person… any study" (§2, §14) and "Ithaca is Gorges" (§10, §14)
  each appear twice.

## 4. Confusing / contradictory framing

- **The cost verdict whiplashes inside one section.** §11 says "near-full-ride", then "NOT loan-free",
  then "weakest funding", then "the grant portion is large". The reader finishes more anxious and less
  clear than they started.
- **Money numbers are vague and mixed.** "~RM 20–50k/yr" is a 2.5× spread; currencies flip between MYR
  and USD with no conversion shown; "mid-1500s SAT" sits next to "A*A*A*A*" with no sense of trade-off.
- **Nested parentheticals are hard to parse** — e.g. "USA / Ithaca, New York (college town (rural
  Finger Lakes, upstate NY))": a bracket inside a bracket, and it already said New York.

## 5. Alphabet soup (unexplained acronyms)

MAE, ECE, CS, ABET, OPT, STEM extension, CSS Profile, JPA-MARA/PKJM, BEM, MQA, Washington Accord, COA,
and the early-round codes (ED / EA / REA / SCEA) are dropped with little or no expansion.

## 6. Critical action items are buried

- **The single most important warning is hidden mid-paragraph.** "An international who does NOT request
  aid at application is permanently barred from applying later" is buried inside a dense §11 among a
  dozen other bolded phrases. It should be an unmissable callout — a skimming student could lose their
  entire financial-aid eligibility by missing it.
- **"Confirm this yourself" tasks are scattered** across five sections (confirm the 4-year English
  record ×2, confirm MQA equivalence, confirm income band) instead of collected into one checklist.

## 7. Where a table / checklist / callout would be far better

| Content | Currently | Should be |
|---|---|---|
| §1 Snapshot | prose bullets with nested brackets | a clean key-value fact table |
| Admit rates (9% overall / 7% RD / 22% ED / intl harder) | buried in a §4 sentence | a small stats table |
| Costs (tuition, COA, net cost, aid structure) | dense §11 prose | a cost table, ideally *vs* the Harvard/Yale/Brown all-grant comparison it keeps making in words |
| Application pieces + deadlines (ED Nov 1 / RD Jan 2, essays, 2 recs + counselor, CSS Profile, fee) | §12 paragraph | a checklist with deadlines |
| Halal dining (Morrison / 104West! / Keeton / Willard Straight) | run-on §9 sentence | a table (location · what's served · hours) |
| §15 Why here / why hesitate | two prose blobs | a two-column pros/cons table |
| "Confirm yourself" items | scattered | one action checklist |

## 8. Structure / smaller issues

- **Sections 5, 6, 7 blur together** — "Academic structure", "Majors & flexibility" and "Signature
  experiences" overlap heavily (the major-affiliation point is explained in both §5 and §6). Candidates
  for merging.
- **The Sources list is noisy** — "— Official — as of 2026" is repeated on all 15 lines. A single
  "as of 2026, official unless marked" note plus a small tag would be cleaner.

---

## Priority fixes (highest impact first)

1. **Add a warning callout** for the "must request aid at application or be barred forever" rule.
2. **Convert the Snapshot, Costs, Admissions and Application sections into tables/checklists.**
3. **Cut the repetition** — state funding, SAT and "apply-to-college" once each; delete or slim §15.
4. **Ease off the bolding** — reserve bold for genuinely key figures and actions.
5. **Switch to second person** ("you") and state the student's ranked priorities once, up front.
6. **Collect all "confirm yourself" items** into a single action checklist.
7. **Expand or footnote acronyms** on first use.
